ActivityEvents and ActivityStreams report on significant events that occur within the application.  The structure described here is patterned heavily after the work of the Activity Stream Work Group.

The central concept is the ActivityEvent.  Every such event is characterized by an actor (who did it), an action (what they did), a date (when they did it), and an object (what was manipulated).  There may also be a target (what got done to).

ActivityEvents are also always Entities (that's not the case with all Events).  That means they also always have a globally unique ID and belong to a particular dataSpace, and by extension a particular tenant.

Actions are represented by Verb entities.  Unlike most of the other entities we will encounter in MindjetGraph, verbs are defined in a well-known central dataSpace: "http://purl.org/sief/2013/10/activity".  The entityKeys for these entities are well-known, published values that may be used to recognize the action.

Verbs are a sub-class of Concept, which means that they have broader and narrower relationships, with other verbs.  This is analogous to the "hypernym" and "hyponym" relationships discussed in  Activity Stream Work Group.  Beware that an activity may have more than one broader concept (aka hypernym), although that is rare.  To be sure, it is rare to write code that dynamically processes these kinds of relationships, but it is there if you need it.

ActivityEvents are delivered by way of ActivityStreams.  A specific activityStream always has a base property that identifies its core organization principle for what belongs in the stream.  Application logic, and potentially complicated logic at that, may come to play to determine which events correspond to a particular base.  ActivityStreams may additionally be associated with a filter that further limits which events belong to the stream.

Note that order and paging are not elements of the data model.  Those features come to play in which ever access mechanism is used, such as the REST API.
